1. WHAT IS THIS PROJECT?
========================

MH-EWSS (Mental Health Early Warning Speech System) is an AI-powered tool that
listens to a person's voice and detects early signs of depression or mental
fatigue—without asking any medical questions. It works purely by analyzing HOW
someone speaks (their vocal physics) and WHAT they say (their word choices).

Think of it like this: when someone is depressed, their voice naturally becomes
flatter, slower, and more monotone. They pause more, use simpler words, and
their vocal energy drops. These are involuntary physiological changes that
happen even when the person is trying to sound "normal." Our system detects
exactly those subtle changes using advanced signal processing and machine
learning.

The system is NOT a medical diagnostic tool. It is an early warning system
that flags potential vocal biomarker deviations and suggests the user seek
professional help if needed.


2. WHY DID WE BUILD THIS?
==========================

Depression is one of the most underdiagnosed conditions globally. Most people
suffering from it never seek help because:
  - They don't realize they are depressed
  - There is social stigma around mental health
  - Access to psychiatrists is limited in many regions

Voice is one of the most accessible biometric signals—everyone has a phone
and can record a short clip of themselves speaking. Research from institutions
like USC (University of Southern California) has shown that vocal biomarkers
can reliably indicate depression severity. Our project takes that research
and builds a practical, usable AI system around it.


3. THE DATASET: DAIC-WOZ
=========================

We train our AI on the DAIC-WOZ (Distress Analysis Interview Corpus) dataset,
developed by USC's Institute for Creative Technologies.

What is it?
  - Real psychiatric clinical interviews (not acted emotions)
  - Each participant sits with a virtual interviewer (an AI avatar named Ellie)
  - The interviews last 10-20 minutes each
  - Participants include both healthy controls and clinically depressed individuals

Why DAIC-WOZ instead of CREMA-D?
  - CREMA-D is an actor-based emotional dataset (people pretending to be sad/angry)
  - A person genuinely suffering from depression sounds completely different from
    an actor faking sadness
  - DAIC-WOZ captures real, involuntary physiological vocal changes
  - This makes our model clinically relevant rather than just an emotion classifier

We currently use 16 participants (300_P through 315_P), generating over 3,000
training samples from their audio recordings.


4. HOW DOES THE AUDIO PROCESSING WORK?
=======================================

Step 1: Audio Chunking
  - Each 15-minute interview is too long to analyze as one piece
  - We slice it into 5-second chunks (e.g., a 15-min file = 180 chunks)
  - Each chunk becomes one independent training sample

Step 2: Acoustic Feature Extraction (23 features per chunk)
  Using the "librosa" Python library, we extract:

  A) 10 Core Prosodic Features:
     1.  Pitch Mean         - Average speaking frequency (Hz)
     2.  Pitch Std          - How monotone vs. varied the voice is
     3.  Energy Mean        - Average loudness/volume
     4.  Energy Std         - Volume dynamics (flat vs. animated)
     5.  Speech Rate        - How fast they speak (syllables/sec)
     6.  Pause Ratio        - Proportion of silence in speech
     7.  HNR                - Harmonics-to-Noise Ratio (voice clarity)
     8.  ZCR Mean           - Zero Crossing Rate (harshness/breathiness)
     9.  Spectral Centroid  - "Brightness" of the voice
     10. Spectral Rolloff   - High-frequency energy distribution

  B) 13 MFCCs (Mel-Frequency Cepstral Coefficients):
     - These are the mathematical "fingerprints" of the vocal tract
     - They represent the physical shape of your mouth, throat, tongue,
       and nasal cavity
     - When depressed, muscle tension drops, which changes these shapes
     - MFCCs capture exactly that change at a sub-conscious level
     - We extract coefficients 1 through 13 (the most informative range)

Step 3: Linguistic Feature Extraction (6 features per chunk)
  Using transcript alignment and TextBlob NLP:
     1. Transcription       - What the person actually said
     2. Word Count          - How many words per chunk
     3. Sentiment Polarity  - Positive vs. negative tone (-1 to +1)
     4. Sentiment Subjectivity - Objective vs. subjective language
     5. Vocabulary Richness - Ratio of unique words to total words
     6. Sentence Complexity - Average words per sentence

Step 4: Clinical Risk Labeling
  Each chunk is automatically labeled as:
    - 0 (Low Risk)    - Normal vocal patterns
    - 1 (Medium Risk) - Some deviation from healthy norms
    - 2 (High Risk)   - Strong indicators of vocal distress

  Labels are assigned based on research-backed thresholds:
    - Very low pitch variability (monotone voice)
    - Extremely high pause ratios (long silences)
    - Very low energy (quiet, withdrawn speech)
    - Negative sentiment in transcribed words

Step 5: Multiprocessing Optimization
  - Processing 16 audio files sequentially with librosa takes hours
  - We use Python's "joblib" library to parallelize the work
  - All CPU cores process different chunks simultaneously (n_jobs=-1)
  - This drops extraction time from 2+ hours to just minutes


5. HOW DOES THE MACHINE LEARNING MODEL WORK?
=============================================

Algorithm: Gradient Boosting Classifier (GBM)
  - An ensemble method that builds hundreds of small decision trees
  - Each new tree corrects the errors of the previous ones
  - Much more accurate than a single Decision Tree or Random Forest
  - Specifically tuned with:
      * 200 estimators (decision trees)
      * Max depth of 4 (prevents overfitting)
      * Learning rate of 0.1 (slow, careful learning)
      * Subsample of 0.8 (uses 80% of data per tree for robustness)

Data Preprocessing: StandardScaler
  - All 23 features have wildly different scales
    (e.g., Pitch is ~200 Hz, but MFCC values are ~0.001)
  - StandardScaler normalizes every feature to have mean=0 and std=1
  - This prevents features with large numbers from dominating the model

Class Balancing: SMOTE (Synthetic Minority Over-sampling Technique)
  - Problem: In real clinical data, most people are NOT severely depressed
    Our raw data: Low Risk = 4.3%, Medium Risk = 71.9%, High Risk = 23.8%
  - If we train directly on this, the AI learns to always guess "Medium Risk"
    and gets 72% accuracy by doing nothing useful
  - SMOTE fixes this by mathematically generating synthetic minority samples
  - How it works:
      1. Pick a real High Risk sample
      2. Find its 5 nearest neighbors (other High Risk samples)
      3. Create a new synthetic sample along the line between them
      4. Repeat until all classes have equal representation
  - After SMOTE: Low = Medium = High = ~1,700 samples each
  - The model now MUST learn the actual differences between classes

Validation: 5-Fold Stratified Cross Validation
  - The data is split into 5 equal parts
  - The model trains on 4 parts and tests on 1 part
  - This repeats 5 times (each part gets to be the test set once)
  - Final accuracy = average of all 5 runs
  - "Stratified" means each fold maintains the same class proportions
  - This gives us a reliable, unbiased estimate of real-world performance

Current Performance:
  - Test Accuracy: ~87-88%
  - 5-Fold CV Accuracy: ~88.56% (+/- 2.01%)
  - The model correctly identifies High Risk cases with ~84% recall


6. THE LANGGRAPH PIPELINE (HOW IT ALL CONNECTS)
================================================

LangGraph is a framework by LangChain that orchestrates AI agents as a
directed graph. Each "node" in the graph is an independent agent that
processes one aspect of the data, then passes its results to the next node.

Our graph has 4 nodes:

Node 1: PROSODY AGENT (extract_features.py)
  Input:  Raw audio file (.wav)
  Output: Dictionary of 23 acoustic features (10 prosodic + 13 MFCCs)
  How:    Uses librosa to compute pitch, energy, speech rate, MFCCs, etc.

Node 2: LANGUAGE AGENT (extract_language_features.py)
  Input:  Raw audio file (.wav)
  Output: Dictionary of 6 linguistic features
  How:    Uses Google Web Speech API to transcribe audio to text,
          then uses TextBlob for sentiment and vocabulary analysis

Node 3: TEMPORAL AGENT (database_manager.py)
  Input:  Features from Nodes 1 and 2
  Output: Personal Baseline comparison data
  How:    Stores all features in SQLite database (ewss_features.db)
          Calculates user's "normal" baseline from historical recordings
          Computes deviation scores (how different today is from your norm)

Node 4: ML + LLM DECISION AGENT (langgraph_workflow.py)
  Input:  All features + baseline + ML model prediction
  Output: Final risk assessment JSON

  This node does TWO things:

  Step A - Local ML Model:
    - Feeds the 23 acoustic features into our trained Gradient Boosting model
    - Gets a precise mathematical probability
      (e.g., "92.3% chance of High Risk")
    - This is pure math, no hallucination possible

  Step B - Gemini LLM Analysis:
    - Takes the ML probability AND all the raw features
    - Constructs a detailed prompt for the Gemini 1.5 Flash API
    - The prompt includes: transcription, vocal physics, baseline deviation,
      AND the ML model's exact probability
    - Gemini returns a structured JSON containing:
        * risk_level: "Low" / "Medium" / "High"
        * explanation: Why this risk level was assigned
        * recommendations: Specific actionable suggestions

  Why use BOTH an ML model AND an LLM?
    - The ML model is precise but can't explain itself in natural language
    - The LLM can explain and recommend but shouldn't do raw math
    - Together: ML provides the mathematical backbone, LLM provides the
      human-readable interpretation and recommendations


7. THE STREAMLIT DASHBOARD (FRONTEND UI)
=========================================

The user interface is built with Streamlit, a Python-based web framework.

What the dashboard shows:
  - Audio upload widget (drag-and-drop any .wav file)
  - Voice Diagnostic Panel:
      * Pitch analysis with historical comparison
      * Energy/loudness analysis
      * Speech rate and pause metrics
  - ML Model Prediction Box:
      * Shows exact probability breakdown (Low %, Medium %, High %)
      * Confidence score
      * Trained dataset indicator (DAIC-WOZ)
  - Mental Health Status Badge:
      * "Healthy" (green) for Low Risk
      * "Needs Attention" (yellow) for Medium Risk
      * "Unhealthy" (red) for High Risk
  - LLM Analysis Section:
      * Gemini's detailed explanation
      * Numbered, actionable recommendations
      * Emergency banner for High Risk cases
  - Historical Trend Charts:
      * Plotly interactive graphs showing feature changes over time
      * Baseline comparison visualization

9. HOW TO RUN THE PROJECT
==========================

Step 1: Install all dependencies
  pip install librosa pandas numpy speechrecognition textblob langgraph
  pip install google-generativeai python-dotenv streamlit plotly
  pip install scikit-learn joblib imbalanced-learn matplotlib seaborn

Step 2: Set up API key
  Open .env file and add:
  GEMINI_API_KEY="your_actual_gemini_api_key"

Step 3: Extract features from DAIC-WOZ audio (uses all CPU cores)
  python source_codes/extract_daic_woz.py

Step 4: Train the Gradient Boosting model with SMOTE
  python source_codes/train_daic_woz_model.py

Step 5: Launch the dashboard
  streamlit run source_codes/app_dashboard.py

Step 6: Upload any .wav audio file and see the full analysis!
